Wellness Architecture

Designing conditions for recovery and sustainable work

I use yoga teaching, mindfulness practice, community operations, and product thinking to study how people recover, connect, and build habits that last. The goal is larger than teaching a class: it is learning how to design practical wellness into everyday systems.

Field Experience

Workplace yoga as a living laboratory

Since May 2024, I have facilitated more than 100 lunchtime workplace yoga sessions for a growing employee community. I design beginner-friendly classes, adapt to limited time and space, and learn from the realities of participation, fatigue, motivation, and group dynamics.

Product Thinking

Small interventions, tested

I treat each program as a service: observe behavior, reduce friction, test a change, and retain what genuinely helps people return.

Design Principles

What I am learning to design for

Low friction

A wellness practice should be easy to enter, especially on a difficult day. The smallest viable intervention is often more useful than an ambitious program nobody can sustain.

Embodied evidence

Surveys matter, but bodies reveal different information. Breath, hesitation, energy, and return behavior are part of the evidence.

Human systems

Wellness is shaped by calendars, managers, space, culture, and social permission. Good intentions need supportive operating systems.

Current Research Question

How can small workplace rituals help people recover?

I am exploring interventions simple enough to survive real work: a short movement break, a shared ritual, a protected calendar moment, or a gentle prompt that makes recovery socially acceptable.
